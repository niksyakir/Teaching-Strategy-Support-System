Teaching Strategies Overview

PERSONALIZED STRATEGIES

No two students are exactly alike. That's why personalized learning strategies builds a learning experience that addresses the unique abilities of each student. Personalized learning allows students to learn without stigma and gets students involved in what they're learning.
See Details

REAL-TIME FEEDBACK

Real-time feedback should not be only a one-way communication from the instructor to the students, but also a collaborative and interactive process among the students and within themselves.

You should encourage peer and self-feedback to foster a sense of community and autonomy among your learners.
See Details

INDEPENDENT LEARNING

Independent learning is an educational approach that encourages students to take charge of their own learning process. By fostering self-reliance, critical thinking, and problem-solving skills, independent learning helps students become more autonomous and responsible learners. This strategy promotes active engagement with content, where students set their own learning goals, manage their time, and seek resources, all of which contribute to deeper understanding and mastery of the subject matter.
See Details

INTERACTIVE LESSONS

Interactive lessons refers to methods of teaching that engage the classroom. Unlike memorization, interactive teaching encourages students and teachers to collaborate to foster learning.

Interactive lessons relies on many methods of interaction to activate the classroom. These include:
1. Interaction between teacher and student
2. Interaction between students and their peers
3. The use of audio, visuals, video
4. Hands-on demonstrations and exercises

FORMATIVE ASSESSMENT

Formative assessments take place during the teaching process.
Formative Assessment
- Occurs through chapter or unit
- Improves how students learn
- Covers small content areas
- Monitors how students are learning
- Focuses on process of student learning

Formative assessments can reduce student stress around testing. They give you the chance to course correct mid-unit if there are serious comprehension issues and to see which students might need a bit more one-on-one time.
See Details

SCAFFOLD INSTRUCTION

Scaffolded instruction is an approach where teachers provide structured support to help students complete tasks they cannot do independently. Over time, as students gain confidence and competence, this support is gradually reduced. This allows students to successfully master tasks and build independence.
See Details

MATH GAMES

Math games can play an active role in getting students engaged in class and excited to learn. Offline or online, they're usually customizable and easy to incorporate into other teaching strategies.

An example of popular math games:
- Bouncing Sums: Label a beach ball with integers, decimals and fractions. Each student must read the number on his or her label, adding or multiplying it by the sum from the previous student.
See Details

READ-ALOUDS LEARNING

When doing a read-aloud, it is best if all students have a copy of the text so that they can follow along, usually taking notes as they listen. The teacher or a volunteer can begin reading the text, reading a few lines or a whole paragraph.

- Teachers can assign students a section of the text to read. Often teachers give students the assignment the night before, so that they can practice reading for homework.
- Read-alouds can be structured “popcorn style.” As soon as one student stops reading, another student can begin.

PEER TEACHING

Peer teaching is a collaborative learning strategy where students take on the role of the teacher, helping their classmates understand and master academic content. This approach fosters a deeper understanding of the material, encourages active learning, and enhances communication skills. By teaching their peers, students reinforce their own knowledge while promoting a sense of community and shared responsibility in the classroom.
See Details

GOAL SETTING

Goal setting is a fundamental aspect of student learning that helps guide progress and provides clear direction for students. By setting specific, measurable, attainable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART) goals, students can focus their efforts, track their progress, and take ownership of their learning. Goal setting empowers students to develop a sense of purpose and motivation, enhancing both academic achievement and personal growth.
